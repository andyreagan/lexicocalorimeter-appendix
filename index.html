Abstract

Upon developing measures of social input and output of "caloric content" in Twitter from text-extracted phrases relating to food and physical activity, we conduct a cross-sectional investigation of the relationships between the food and physical activity phrases used on Twitter and state-level measures of health and well-being. We find that our measures of "calories in," "calories out," and the ratio of these measures called "caloric balance" are strong geospatial correlates with health and well-being demographics. Our "caloric balance" measure is a useful proxy for a state's overall health and well-being that has the potential to be used alongside population survey data and more traditional public health measures in analyzing health on a population level.
